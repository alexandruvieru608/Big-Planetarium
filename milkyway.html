Our Home Galaxy

A barred spiral galaxy 100,000 light-years wide, containing over 200 billion stars — including our very own Sun, located about 26,000 light-years from the galactic center.

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy with four major spiral arms: the Perseus Arm, Scutum-Centaurus Arm, Sagittarius Arm, and Norma Arm. Our solar system sits in a minor arm called the Orion Spur, between the Perseus and Sagittarius arms. The central bar of the galaxy is a dense region of stars about 27,000 light-years long, and at its very center lies the supermassive black hole Sagittarius A*.

Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*) is the supermassive black hole at the heart of the Milky Way. In May 2022, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first image of it — a glowing ring of gas and dust surrounding the invisible black hole. Despite its enormous mass (4 million times the Sun), Sgr A* is currently in a relatively quiet state compared to active galactic nuclei in other galaxies.

Our solar system lies roughly 26,000 light-years from the galactic center, in a quiet region of the Orion Spur — a minor arm connecting the Perseus and Sagittarius Arms. The Sun orbits the galactic center at about 828,000 km/h, completing one orbit (a "galactic year") every 225–250 million years. Since the Sun formed 4.6 billion years ago, it has completed about 20 orbits around the Milky Way's core.

Milkomeda

  • New Galaxy Name"Milkomeda"
  • Resulting ShapeElliptical galaxy
  • Star CollisionsExtremely unlikely
  • Our SunWill survive the merger

In about 4–5 billion years, the Milky Way and the Andromeda Galaxy will begin to collide and eventually merge into a single, large elliptical galaxy that astronomers have nicknamed "Milkomeda." Despite the dramatic scale of this event, individual stars are so widely spaced that actual stellar collisions are extremely unlikely. Our Sun should survive the merger, though it will likely be thrown into a different orbit within the new galaxy.

History

History of Discovery

1610

Galileo's Telescope

Galileo Galilei used his telescope to resolve the Milky Way into individual stars, disproving earlier beliefs that it was a cloud.

1785

William Herschel's Map

Herschel created the first attempt at a map of the Milky Way by counting stars in different directions, estimating it to be lens-shaped.

1920

The Great Debate

Astronomers Shapley and Curtis debated whether nebulae were inside our galaxy or separate "island universes." Curtis was right.

1958

Spiral Structure Confirmed

Radio astronomy revealed the spiral arm structure of the Milky Way, showing our galaxy's true barred spiral form.

2022

First Image of Sagittarius A*

The Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first direct image of the Milky Way's supermassive black hole.
